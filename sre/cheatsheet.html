SLI / SLO / SLA / Error Budget

  • SLI — a precise, quantitative measurement (e.g. good events / total events), not a vague health sense
  • SLO — a target attached to an SLI over a window, e.g. "99.9% of requests succeed over rolling 28 days"
  • SLA — an SLO wrapped in a contract; missing it triggers a formal (often financial) consequence, unlike an internal SLO miss
  • Error budget = 100% − SLO — derived, not separately measured; a 99.9% SLO leaves a 0.1% budget
  • Error budget policy: while budget remains, ship normally; once exhausted, freeze non-critical releases until back within target
  • Treat the SLO as both a floor and a ceiling — running far above it just trains users/dependents to expect more than promised

Nines & Availability

TargetShorthand
99%2 nines
99.95%3.5 nines
99.99%4 nines

99.95% = Google Compute Engine's own published target. 99.99% allows ~52 min/yr (~13 min/quarter) downtime. Each extra nine costs disproportionately more for shrinking user-perceptible benefit — most users can't tell 99.99% from 99.999% apart. Aggregate (request-based) availability = successful / total requests over a window, not simple up/down time.

Burn-Rate Alerting

  • Burn rate 1 = current error rate would exhaust the whole budget exactly at window's end; burn rate 10 = 10× faster (budget gone in 1/10 the window)
  • Multiwindow, multi-burn-rate catches fast and slow burns; a short-window confirmation (~1/12 the long window) clears the alert quickly once resolved
  • 99.9% SLO starting thresholds: 14.4× sustained over 1h (+5min confirm) → page; 6× over 6h (+30min confirm) → page; 1× over 3 days (+6h confirm) → ticket
  • Breaks down at both traffic extremes: very low traffic (one failure = huge % burn) and very high reliability targets (99.999% burns a month's budget in ~26s)

Four Golden Signals

  • Latency — track failed-request latency separately from successful (a fast failure hides a slow, damaging one)
  • Traffic — demand in whatever unit fits (req/s, sessions, tx/s)
  • Errors — a 200 status isn't proof of success (wrong payload, blown latency bound)
  • Saturation — how full the tightest constraining resource is, not a generic CPU%; forward-looking (e.g. disk fills in N hours)

Monitoring Types

  • Black-box — tests externally visible behavior like a user would; symptom-oriented, only catches problems already happening — good for paging (low false positives)
  • White-box — reads internals (logs, counters); can catch trouble before it's user-visible, including retries silently masking failures
  • Metrics — compact numeric series, near-real-time → alerts & dashboards
  • Logs — rich per-event detail, batch-lag → root-cause investigation

MTTR / MTBF

  • MTTR — mean time to repair: once broken, how long to detect, diagnose & fully resolve. Playbooks cut MTTR ~3× vs. improvising
  • MTBF — mean time between failures: the average stretch of working time between one failure and the next, i.e. how rarely a system breaks in the first place (not "before failing")
  • A system can have great MTBF and terrible MTTR, or vice versa — they describe different halves of an incident's timeline

Incident Roles (ICS-derived)

  • Incident Commander — tracks overall state, delegates roles, defaults to covering any role nobody's claimed
  • Operations lead — the only one meant to actually change the system live
  • Communications lead — public face, keeps the living incident doc & stakeholders updated on a cadence
  • Planning lead — bugs, handoffs, tracking drift from normal state (SRE book's 4th role; the Workbook's own "3 Cs" framing names only 3 core roles)
  • Mitigate first, understand second — assess impact → mitigate (e.g. "generic mitigation": rollback/reroute) → root-cause later in the postmortem

On-Call & Escalation

  • Response-time target derives from the SLO's own error budget, not habit — typical: 5 min (user-facing/critical) vs. 30 min (looser systems)
  • ~2 incidents/12h shift is a staffing heuristic (full incident lifecycle averages ~6h), not a refusal threshold; median incidents/shift should sit near zero
  • Shifts: keep to ~12h if paging at least daily — a full 24h stretch degrades judgment
  • Primary answers every page; secondary's job (fallback-only vs. ticket-absorbing) varies by team; escalation is normal, not failure

Postmortems

  • Blameless = assume good faith, not "no consequences" — still needs owned, tracked action items (accountability moves to fixes, not people)
  • Required triggers: user-visible outage/degradation past a threshold, any data loss, a manual on-call intervention (rollback/reroute), overlong resolution, or a monitoring gap that let it go undetected
  • Strong action item = one named owner, a linked bug, a priority, and a checkable done-state — "train people to be careful" is a weak action item vs. fixing the system

Release Patterns

  • Canary — a genuine A/B test: small treatment slice vs. control on the still-running old version, not just a safety net; needs traffic-split + an automated verdict + pipeline integration
  • A 20%-failure release confined to a 5% canary produces ~1% overall failure — canarying conserves error budget by scaling down blast radius
  • After canary passes: progressive rollout continues in stages (capacity, then region) — blast radius stays bounded the whole way, not just at the first stage
  • Blue/green — two full environments, zero-downtime cutover, instant revert by flipping traffic back; roughly doubles infrastructure footprint
  • Rollback first, diagnose second — reverting to known-good is faster/safer than patching live under incident pressure; ~70% of outages trace to changes to a live system

Reliability Patterns & Toil

  • Toil = manual + repetitive + automatable + tactical + no enduring value + scales O(n) with the service — disliking work or doing it once doesn't make it toil
  • N+2 — survive your two largest deployments lost at once (one planned drain + one unplanned failure) at peak load
  • Quorum (2f+1) — tolerates f simultaneous failures; 3 replicas tolerate 1, 5 tolerate 2 (the recommended floor for anything intolerant of an outage)
  • Cascading failure — positive feedback where one failure raises the odds of the next (not just several things breaking at once)
  • Criticality tiers, most→least protected: CRITICAL_PLUS > CRITICAL > SHEDDABLE_PLUS > SHEDDABLE — sheds lowest tiers first under overload
